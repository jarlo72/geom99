GEOM99 Group Project

Topic Selection

Our project centers on the Vancouver Island Trail on Vancouver Island in British Columbia. The trail is currently under construction and is being promoted and monitored by the Vancouver Island Trail Association (aka VITA for short). Some members of or group are working with VITA for their final collaborative work, and this project is a first blush of web map possibilities for the trail.

Initial Overview

VITA's website contains images of maps; there are no active interfaces for users and no capabilities for hikers to investigate sections of trails for possible hikes. VITA2022 therefore decided to focus on providing interactive web maps. As described below, our first thought was to do a web map with wildlife sightings and a link to weather forecasts. From there we proceeded to trying a second idea and third iteration.

First Concept

The initial concept floated was a web-based GIS solution incorporating open-source data for both wildlife sightings and weather forecasts to make an informative and accessible web map of the Vancouver Island Trail. A map, possibly derived from either Google Maps JavaScript API or Leaflet, would include pop-up information on geo-referenced wildlife sightings from iNaturalist, or possibly weather forecasts from OpenWeather Live. Base mapping data may also be sourced from Open Street Maps.
Upon review of Google Maps, however, we realized the potential cost to our non-profit client would be prohibitive, so our focus shifted to our second thought, which was an alternative mapping option.

Second Concept

We focused on using either Esri Leaflet or MapBox for the base map, stored on an ArcGIS server. However, it was pointed out to us that the project scope was too large and we were advised to pull back our ambitions. This led us to our third iteration as outlined below.

Third Concept

The trail data was extracted from VITA's website in GPX format, which was summarily brought into ArcGIS Pro and processed. A DEM was downloaded from the the federal government's GeoGratis portal as it had more complete elevation data than the information on the VITA website. It too was added to ArcGIS Pro. The group decided to focus on the south portion of the trail. The resulting map was published to Fleming's AGOL server.
Drawing on trail guide information extracted as .pdf data from VITA's website, an ArcGIS StoryMap was generated and published to the AGOL server to give users a guided tour of the south section of the trail. But the team was not satisfied with only one possible solution. This led us to our fourth iteration, another Esri product.

Fourth Concept

A second web-based map was created using ArcGIS API for JavaScript; this was also published to AGOL. This map allows users to carry out self-guided tours of the south sections of the trail. Numerous widgets were added:

  • ability to change base imagery
  • ability to change the zoom scale
  • ability to pan and rotate in 3D
  • ability to reset the map orientation
  • ability to track the user's location
  • ability to create custom trail profiles
